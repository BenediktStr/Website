# Are Certifications Worth It?

A few days ago I passed the Microsoft Azure Administrator Exam after roughly 40 hours of studying. I use Azure on a daily basis in my day job, so I thought it would be a nice portfolio addition. But every day I studied, I thought: "Shouldn't I improve my real-world skills instead of passing some artificial exam?" It's only a certification, not that important, right?

So is chasing certifications and degrees worth it, or do capable people simply not need external proof? The answer is not that obvious, so let's dive in...

"A certificate proves you are capable, and also proves you are not capable at all." What do I mean by that?

A big part of job culture is showing others that you can do certain things, whether it means being familiar with a piece of software or simply having something to show for. But everyone knows deep down that a certificate proves a certain thing and, at the same time, proves nothing at all.

Mastery of a skill, a piece of software, or a field of knowledge is a core part of our efforts to prove we ARE capable. We want to prove it to ourselves, and also to others.

Let's dissect both sides:


## Why It Matters

You need to communicate what you're capable of. A certification is often the easiest way to do that. In many fields you can't just point to a finished product, and even if you could, there is no guarantee you actually built it yourself. A certification, whether it's a full degree or a smaller exam like mine, is a quick way to present capability on a topic that would otherwise be hard to demonstrate in a few moments.

There is also a practical reality on the other side of the table. The person deciding which candidate gets the role doesn't have infinite time or resources to make the perfect decision, quite the opposite. On top of that, there is the **accountability bias**: if the hiring manager picks the "safer" candidate with credentials and that person works out, fine. If they pick the riskier candidate with great ideas but no proof on paper, and that person fails, they need more than "I had a good feeling about him" to justify the decision. The upside of taking the risk is small, but the downside is personal. Certifications make the decision easier for everyone involved.



## Why It Doesn't Matter

Anyone with a certification knows that plenty of people without any formal proof are often far more capable than those with one. A certification doesn't fill the gap between *saying* you know something and *actually* knowing it. And there is a diminishing return: collect too many certs and it starts to look like you're trying too hard to impress, like cosplaying as the expert you are not. It's like buying expensive photography equipment with only basic skills: people will expect more from you, not less. At the end, a certification is a door opener, not permission to stay for the long run.

## My Take

On the other side, passing the exam felt nice, like collecting a gym badge in Pokémon and moving one step forward. And maybe that's where it clicked for me: in fields with a structured path toward a goal, certifications can be one of the steps along the way.

So what's my take? If you can prove your skills through actual work —> great, maybe you don't need one. But in many fields, mine included, that's not always easy. A respected certification paired with real projects is the most practical way to maximize both your plausibility and your employability.

I'm on my journey to sharpen my DevOps skills, and I'll keep collecting the badges I need along the way. See you after the next one —> AZ-400.
